Kingman's VUT Simulator — Methodology & User Guide

Capacity methodology dashboard Kingman's VUT Simulator — Methodology & User Guide What this models The simulator models a single-server queue — one supplier workstation processing a stream of incoming part orders, some from The Company  and some from other customers. It answers a deceptively simple question: given how busy this supplier is and how variable their arrivals and service times are, how long will a part wait before processing even begins? That wait time — the queue wait, not the processing time itself — is what drives lead time unpredictability in supply chains.

The core equation: Kingman's VUT formula The mathematical backbone is Kingman's approximation for the mean waiting time in a G/G/1 queue (a general single-server queue with general arrival and service distributions): Wait = ((CVₐ² + CVₛ²) / 2) × ProcessingTime × u / (1 − u) This breaks into three intuitive factors, which is why it's so useful for building intuition: V — Variability factor: (CVₐ² + CVₛ²) / 2 The coefficient of variation (CV) measures how erratic a process is relative to its average. CVₐ captures variability in when parts arrive; CVₛ captures variability in how long each part takes to process. If both arrivals and service were perfectly regular (CV = 0), there would be no queue at all — parts would flow through like clockwork. Real suppliers have neither, so variability creates idle gaps and bunching that force parts to wait. U — Utilization factor: u / (1 − u) This term is responsible for the most important insight in the model. As utilization u approaches 1 (100%), the denominator approaches zero and wait time explodes toward infinity. At 50% utilization the multiplier is 1×; at 80% it's 4×; at 90% it's 9×; at 95% it's 19×. This nonlinearity is why suppliers who appear to have "only 10% spare capacity" are often chronically late — they are operating on the near-vertical part of the curve. T — Processing time Wait scales linearly with how long each job takes. A supplier processing large structural assemblies over many hours will accumulate far longer queues than one processing small fasteners in minutes, even at identical utilization and variability.

The simulation engine The VUT formula is an approximation. The simulator also runs a discrete-event simulation (DES) alongside it so you can see how accurate that approximation is and observe dynamics the formula cannot capture. The simulation works as follows:

Generate arrivals. 800 jobs are created with inter-arrival times drawn from a statistical distribution matching the CVₐ you set. For low CVₐ (regular arrivals), it uses an Erlang distribution; for high CVₐ (bursty arrivals), exponential draws. Label each job. Each arriving job is independently tagged The Company  or non-The Company  based on the mix percentage you set. Process the queue. Jobs enter a single-server FCFS (first-come, first-served) queue. When the server is free, the next job starts immediately; when it's busy, the job waits. Service times are drawn from the same distributional family as arrivals, parameterized by CVₛ. Record outcomes. For every job the simulation records arrival time, service start time, and completion time, from which wait time = start − arrival is derived. The Company  and non-The Company  waits are tracked separately. Aggregate and visualize. The simulation produces average waits, a wait time distribution, and queue length snapshots over time.

The simulation re-runs every time you move a slider, so charts update live.

Why The Company and non-The Company waits converge: An important result you'll notice: The Company  parts don't consistently wait more or less than other parts. This is because the model uses a shared FCFS queue with no priority discipline. In a purely shared queue, job type is irrelevant to wait time — what matters is the state of the server when your job arrives, which is random. The The Company /non-The Company  comparison in this model therefore illustrates the baseline — what The Company  experiences when it has no preferential treatment at all. To model priority queuing (The Company  jobs jump the queue), a different queuing discipline would be needed.

The four charts explained Wait time vs. utilization — The classic hockey-stick curve. This is the single most important chart: it shows that the relationship between utilization and wait is not linear. Most of the damage happens in the last 15% of capacity. Wait time vs. variability — Sweeps CVₐ and CVₛ independently while holding everything else fixed. This shows that variability and utilization are multiplicative — high variability at low utilization is manageable, but high variability at high utilization is catastrophic. VUT vs. simulation comparison — Plots both estimates across utilization levels. The VUT formula is generally accurate for moderate utilization and CV ≈ 1 (exponential-like behavior). It tends to diverge at very high utilization or extreme CV values, where the formula's approximation assumptions break down. Queue length over time — Shows the simulated backlog as jobs accumulate and drain across the run. At low utilization, the queue stays near zero. At high utilization, it grows steadily or oscillates with periodic surges — a pattern that resembles real supplier backlogs.

What this tells The Company: Three operational takeaways emerge directly from the model: Utilization is the multiplier, not just a metric. A supplier running at 90% isn't "10% away from full" — they are 9× more exposed to delays than one running at 50%. Reducing a key supplier from 92% to 85% utilization can cut expected wait times by more than half. Variability reduction compounds with capacity. Investments in smoothing arrival schedules (more consistent release of purchase orders) or reducing service variability (standardized work, fewer rework loops) have outsized returns precisely when utilization is already high. Lead time averages are misleading. The wait distribution histogram shows that even when the average wait is moderate, the tail of the distribution — the worst 10–20% of orders — can be several times longer. For The Company , it is those tail events that cause line-down situations, not the average.
